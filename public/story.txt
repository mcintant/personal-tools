On the Great White Way, where marquees glittered like a carillon of light, a young tyro named Mina carried a threadbare valise and an idée fixe: she would become a writer.

She’d come from a small, parochial town with a bucolic calm—too calm, lately, for her ardour. New York felt like the acme of everything: ambition, noise, possibility. Even the street vendors’ cries seemed vociferously hopeful.

That night, she ducked into a narrow theater between a furrier and a shop selling cheap baubles. A sign promised a “one-night revue,” and Mina, feeling languid from travel, slipped inside. In the lobby, an obsequious usher with a glossy smile tried to steer her toward an overpriced seat. Mina demurred—politely, but firmly—and took a cheaper chair in the back.

The show began with ostentation—feathered costumes, glitter, a joke so jocular it nearly shook the balcony. Then, with no warning, the tone shifted. A spotlight found an old woman in plain gabardine, alone onstage, reading a letter in an epistolary voice that felt startlingly intimate.

“I write,” the woman said, “to allay the disquiet that has beclouded you.”

Mina sat up. The words felt aimed at her, as if the performer had divined her thoughts.

The letter described a talented young man who’d absconded from his responsibilities—vanishing not from danger, but from discomfort. “He feared the opprobrium of failure,” the woman read, “so he chose the safety of disappearing.”

Mina flinched. Her own family hadn’t been cruel, exactly, but their reproaches had rankled: What’s your plan? How will you live? She’d left anyway, letting certainty sit in abeyance while she chased a dream.

After the curtain call, Mina lingered by the stage door. The old woman emerged, her mien calm and magisterial, as if she carried her own weather.

“You’re the one who wrote that,” Mina blurted, incautious.

The woman’s smile was laconic. “I adduced a few truths,” she said. “Truth travels well.”

“What should I do?” Mina asked, suddenly aware of her own diffidence. “I’m… I don’t want to be a fraud.”

“Fraud is too Machiavellian a word for an honest fear,” the woman said, and her tone held a small clemency. “You’re just a beginner. A tyro with good instincts.”

Mina nodded, though consternation still buzzed in her chest. Outside, the night air was sharp, and the city’s welter of sound pressed in—taxis, laughter, someone arguing, someone singing. It could have been bedlam, but it wasn’t. Not really.

“Walk with me,” the woman said, and they stepped into the flow of people as if joining a phalanx.

They passed a church with open doors. Inside, a small choir practiced a doxology, their voices rising in a simple pattern that made Mina’s shoulders loosen. It wasn’t her faith, exactly, but the music offered a modicum of steadiness.

“You see,” the woman said, “even a city has moments of equanimity.”

Mina looked up. Above the rooftops, the firmament was mostly hidden by haze, but one bright star pricked through. For a moment, the world seemed Edenic—not perfect, not innocent, but fresh, like something newly possible.

“And if I fail?” Mina asked.

The woman gave a small, wryly amused exhale. “Then you ameliorate,” she said. “You revise. You try again. If you let fear rule you, you’ll become torpid—and nothing grows from torpor.”

Mina’s idée fixe didn’t vanish. It changed shape. It became less like a dream she could lose, and more like work she could do—assiduously, day by day, in the quotidian hours no one applauded.

When they reached the corner, the woman tipped her hat—she practically doffed it with old-fashioned grace.

“Go on,” she said. “Write something true. Even if it’s small.”

Mina walked on alone, no longer crestfallen, her steps lighter, as if she’d been quietly extricated from her own doubt. The Great White Way blazed ahead, and for once, it didn’t look like judgment.

It looked like a page.
